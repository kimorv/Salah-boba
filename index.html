Hay mohammadi and the Green March

Hay Mohammadi, a major popular neighborhood in Casablanca, is historically significant for its role in anti-colonial struggles and later internal political dissent, but it does not have a direct, specific, or unique connection to the Green March that is distinct from the general national participation. The Green March was a mass mobilization that involved 350,000 civilian volunteers from all regions of Morocco who converged on the southern city of Tarfaya before crossing into the Spanish Sahara in November 1975 to assert Moroccan sovereignty over the territory. Hay Mohammadi Historical Context Anti-colonial Hub: During the colonial era, Hay Mohammadi was a significant center for industrial and housing innovation and played a crucial role in the anti-colonial struggle. Post-Colonial Dissent: After independence, the neighborhood was known as a center of political contestation and protests against the monarch's power, particularly during the reign of King Hassan II. These protests sometimes led to violent state repression, such as the major uprising in 1981 related to price increases. The Green March Context National Unity Event: The Green March was characterized by its ability to unite Moroccans "from the northern mountains to the southern plains" in a shared nationalist impulse to reclaim the Sahara peacefully. Broad Participation: Volunteers included men and women from all walks of life—farmers, students, teachers, and shopkeepers—who registered in local offices across the country and traveled south. Lack of Specific Link: While it's highly probable some residents of Hay Mohammadi, like other Moroccans, participated in the general call for volunteers, historical sources do not point to the neighborhood as having a specific or leading role in organizing or executing the Green March itself. Its primary historical notability in national narratives often relates more to internal political dynamics and labor movements than this specific territorial dispute event. In essence, the Green March was a national initiative drawing from the entire country, whereas Hay Mohammadi's historical identity is more rooted in its local social and political activism in Casablanca.
